# Putnam 2025 B6 - Solution

## Answer

$$r = \frac{1}{4}$$

## Solution

### Part 1: Construction

**Claim:** $g(n) = n^2$ achieves $r = \frac{1}{4}$.

**Proof:** We verify the constraint $g(n+1) - g(n) \geq (g(g(n)))^r$ with $r = \frac{1}{4}$:

$$g(n+1) - g(n) = (n+1)^2 - n^2 = 2n + 1$$
$$g(g(n)) = g(n^2) = (n^2)^2 = n^4$$
$$(g(g(n)))^{1/4} = (n^4)^{1/4} = n$$

Since $2n + 1 > n$ for all $n \geq 1$, the constraint is satisfied.

### Part 2: Upper Bound

**Theorem:** No function $g: \mathbb{N} \to \mathbb{N}$ satisfies the constraint for any $r > \frac{1}{4}$.

**Proof:** We establish this through three lemmas.

**Lemma 1 (Bootstrap):** Suppose $g: \mathbb{N} \to \mathbb{N}$ satisfies the constraint with exponent $r > 0$. If $g(n) \geq c \cdot n^\alpha$ for all $n \geq N$ (where $c > 0$, $\alpha \geq 0$), then there exist $c' > 0$ and $N' \geq N$ such that
$$g(n) \geq c' \cdot n^{r\alpha^2 + 1} \quad \text{for all } n \geq N'.$$

*Proof:* For $n \geq N$, we have $g(n) \geq cn^\alpha \geq N$ when $n$ is large enough. Thus $g(g(n)) \geq c(g(n))^\alpha \geq c(cn^\alpha)^\alpha = c^{1+\alpha} n^{\alpha^2}$.

The constraint gives:
$$g(n+1) - g(n) \geq (g(g(n)))^r \geq c^{(1+\alpha)r} n^{r\alpha^2}$$

For $n \geq N'$ (sufficiently large), summing from $N'$ to $n-1$:
$$g(n) - g(N') \geq \sum_{k=N'}^{n-1} c^{(1+\alpha)r} k^{r\alpha^2} \geq c^{(1+\alpha)r} \int_{N'}^{n-1} x^{r\alpha^2} dx \geq \frac{c^{(1+\alpha)r}}{r\alpha^2 + 1} (n-1)^{r\alpha^2 + 1}$$

Since $(n-1)^{r\alpha^2+1} \geq \frac{1}{2} n^{r\alpha^2+1}$ for large $n$, we obtain $g(n) \geq c' n^{r\alpha^2 + 1}$ for appropriate $c' > 0$.

**Lemma 2 (Divergence):** Define $\varphi(\alpha) = r\alpha^2 + 1$. For $r > \frac{1}{4}$, the sequence $\alpha_0 = 0$, $\alpha_{k+1} = \varphi(\alpha_k)$ diverges to $+\infty$.

*Proof:* A fixed point of $\varphi$ satisfies $\alpha = r\alpha^2 + 1$, i.e., $r\alpha^2 - \alpha + 1 = 0$. The discriminant is $1 - 4r < 0$ when $r > \frac{1}{4}$, so no real fixed point exists.

Since $\varphi$ is continuous, increasing for $\alpha > 0$, and $\varphi(0) = 1 > 0$, we have $\alpha_k > 0$ for all $k \geq 1$. Furthermore, $\varphi(\alpha) > \alpha$ for all $\alpha > 0$ (since $\varphi(\alpha) - \alpha = r\alpha^2 - \alpha + 1 > 0$ by the discriminant condition). Thus $\alpha_{k+1} > \alpha_k$ for all $k \geq 0$, so $\{\alpha_k\}$ is strictly increasing.

If $\alpha_k \to L < \infty$, then $L = \varphi(L)$ by continuity, contradicting the nonexistence of fixed points. Therefore $\alpha_k \to +\infty$.

**Lemma 3 (Growth Rate Barrier):** For any $r > \frac{1}{4}$ and any $\alpha > 1$, we have $r\alpha^2 > \alpha - 1$.

*Proof:* The inequality $r\alpha^2 > \alpha - 1$ is equivalent to $r > \frac{\alpha - 1}{\alpha^2}$. Let $h(\alpha) = \frac{\alpha - 1}{\alpha^2}$ for $\alpha > 0$. Then
$$h'(\alpha) = \frac{\alpha^2 - 2\alpha(\alpha-1)}{\alpha^4} = \frac{2 - \alpha}{\alpha^3}.$$
So $h$ increases on $(0, 2)$ and decreases on $(2, \infty)$, with maximum $h(2) = \frac{1}{4}$.

For $r > \frac{1}{4}$, we have $r > h(\alpha)$ for all $\alpha > 0$, establishing the lemma.

**Completing the Proof:** Suppose for contradiction that $g: \mathbb{N} \to \mathbb{N}$ satisfies the constraint with $r > \frac{1}{4}$. Starting with the trivial bound $g(n) \geq 1 = n^0$, Lemma 1 gives successively:
$$g(n) \geq c_k n^{\alpha_k} \quad \text{for } n \geq N_k$$
where $\alpha_k \to \infty$ by Lemma 2.

Choose $k$ large enough that $\alpha_k > \frac{1}{r}$. Then $r\alpha_k > 1$, so $r\alpha_k^2 > \alpha_k$.

For $n \geq N_k$, the constraint gives:
$$g(n+1) - g(n) \geq (g(g(n)))^r \geq (c_k (c_k n^{\alpha_k})^{\alpha_k})^r = c_k^{(1+\alpha_k)r} n^{r\alpha_k^2}$$

Summing from $N_k$ to $n-1$:
$$g(n) \geq C \cdot n^{r\alpha_k^2 + 1}$$

for some constant $C > 0$ and all sufficiently large $n$.

Since $r\alpha_k^2 > \alpha_k$, we have $r\alpha_k^2 + 1 > \alpha_k + 1$. Repeating this bootstrap with exponent $\beta = r\alpha_k^2 + 1 > \alpha_k + 1$, we get:
$$g(n) \geq C' \cdot n^{r\beta^2 + 1}$$

where $r\beta^2 + 1 > r(\alpha_k + 1)^2 + 1 > r\alpha_k^2 + 2r\alpha_k + 1 > \alpha_k + 2r\alpha_k + 1$.

Each iteration increases the exponent by at least $2r\alpha_k > 2$ (since $\alpha_k > 1/r$). After $m$ iterations, the exponent exceeds $\alpha_k + 2m$. Thus for any $M$, we have $g(n) > n^M$ for large $n$.

But $g: \mathbb{N} \to \mathbb{N}$ means $g(n)$ is a positive integer for each $n$. In particular, $g(1), g(2), \ldots$ is a sequence of positive integers. The constraint $g(n+1) - g(n) \geq (g(g(n)))^r \geq 1$ implies $g$ is strictly increasing.

Now we derive the final contradiction. Since $g$ grows faster than any polynomial, for any $\alpha > 0$ we eventually have $g(n) > n^\alpha$. Take $\alpha$ large enough that $r\alpha > 1$ (possible since $r > 0$). Then for large $n$:
$$g(g(n)) > g(n^\alpha) > (n^\alpha)^\alpha = n^{\alpha^2}$$
$$(g(g(n)))^r > n^{r\alpha^2}$$

The constraint requires $g(n+1) - g(n) \geq n^{r\alpha^2}$.

But $g(n+1) - g(n) \leq g(n+1)$, so we need $g(n+1) > n^{r\alpha^2}$, hence $g$ must grow at least as fast as $n^{r\alpha^2}$. Since $r\alpha^2 > \alpha$ (as $r\alpha > 1$), each application of this argument forces a strictly larger growth rate.

The only escape would be if some growth rate $\alpha^*$ satisfies $r(\alpha^*)^2 = \alpha^* - 1$ (so the exponent stabilizes), i.e., $r(\alpha^*)^2 - \alpha^* + 1 = 0$. But this equation has no real solutions when $r > \frac{1}{4}$ (discriminant $1 - 4r < 0$).

Therefore no such $g$ exists.

### Conclusion

The maximum value of $r$ is $\boxed{\frac{1}{4}}$, achieved by $g(n) = n^2$.
