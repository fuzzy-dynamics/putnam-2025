# Putnam 2025 B2 - Solution

## Answer

$x_1 < x_2$

## Solution

### Step 1: Formulas for the Centroids

The $x$-coordinate of the centroid of the 2D region $R$ is:
$$x_1 = \frac{\int_0^1 x \cdot f(x) \, dx}{\int_0^1 f(x) \, dx}$$

For the solid of revolution about the $x$-axis, the volume element at position $x$ is $dV = \pi [f(x)]^2 dx$. The $x$-coordinate of the centroid is:
$$x_2 = \frac{\int_0^1 x \cdot \pi [f(x)]^2 \, dx}{\int_0^1 \pi [f(x)]^2 \, dx} = \frac{\int_0^1 x [f(x)]^2 \, dx}{\int_0^1 [f(x)]^2 \, dx}$$

### Step 2: Setting Up the Inequality

We need to prove:
$$\frac{\int_0^1 x f(x) \, dx}{\int_0^1 f(x) \, dx} < \frac{\int_0^1 x [f(x)]^2 \, dx}{\int_0^1 [f(x)]^2 \, dx}$$

Since $f$ is strictly increasing and continuous on $[0,1]$ mapping to $[0,\infty)$, both denominators are positive. Cross-multiplying:
$$\left(\int_0^1 x f(x) \, dx\right) \left(\int_0^1 [f(x)]^2 \, dx\right) < \left(\int_0^1 x [f(x)]^2 \, dx\right) \left(\int_0^1 f(x) \, dx\right)$$

### Step 3: The Proof

We will prove the equivalent statement:
$$\left(\int_0^1 x [f(x)]^2 \, dx\right) \left(\int_0^1 f(x) \, dx\right) - \left(\int_0^1 x f(x) \, dx\right) \left(\int_0^1 [f(x)]^2 \, dx\right) > 0$$

Consider the double integral:
$$I = \int_0^1 \int_0^1 (y - x) f(x) f(y) [f(y) - f(x)] \, dx \, dy$$

**Claim:** $I > 0$.

**Proof of Claim:** For any $(x, y) \in [0,1]^2$:
- If $x < y$: then $(y - x) > 0$ and $f(y) - f(x) > 0$ (since $f$ is strictly increasing), so $(y-x)[f(y) - f(x)] > 0$
- If $x > y$: then $(y - x) < 0$ and $f(y) - f(x) < 0$, so $(y-x)[f(y) - f(x)] > 0$
- If $x = y$: then $(y - x)[f(y) - f(x)] = 0$

Since $f$ is continuous and not identically zero, $f(x)f(y) > 0$ on a set of positive measure. Therefore the integrand is strictly positive on a set of positive measure, giving $I > 0$.

**Expanding $I$:**
$$I = \int_0^1 \int_0^1 (y - x) f(x) f(y) [f(y) - f(x)] \, dx \, dy$$
$$= \int_0^1 \int_0^1 (y - x) [f(x) f(y)^2 - f(x)^2 f(y)] \, dx \, dy$$

Separating the terms:
$$I = \int_0^1 \int_0^1 y f(x) f(y)^2 \, dx \, dy - \int_0^1 \int_0^1 x f(x) f(y)^2 \, dx \, dy$$
$$\quad - \int_0^1 \int_0^1 y f(x)^2 f(y) \, dx \, dy + \int_0^1 \int_0^1 x f(x)^2 f(y) \, dx \, dy$$

Using Fubini's theorem to separate the integrals:
$$I = \left(\int_0^1 y f(y)^2 \, dy\right)\left(\int_0^1 f(x) \, dx\right) - \left(\int_0^1 x f(x) \, dx\right)\left(\int_0^1 f(y)^2 \, dy\right)$$
$$\quad - \left(\int_0^1 y f(y) \, dy\right)\left(\int_0^1 f(x)^2 \, dx\right) + \left(\int_0^1 x f(x)^2 \, dx\right)\left(\int_0^1 f(y) \, dy\right)$$

The first and second terms give:
$$\left(\int_0^1 x f^2 \, dx\right)\left(\int_0^1 f \, dx\right) - \left(\int_0^1 x f \, dx\right)\left(\int_0^1 f^2 \, dx\right)$$

The third and fourth terms give:
$$-\left(\int_0^1 x f \, dx\right)\left(\int_0^1 f^2 \, dx\right) + \left(\int_0^1 x f^2 \, dx\right)\left(\int_0^1 f \, dx\right)$$

Therefore:
$$I = 2\left[\left(\int_0^1 x f(x)^2 \, dx\right)\left(\int_0^1 f(x) \, dx\right) - \left(\int_0^1 x f(x) \, dx\right)\left(\int_0^1 f(x)^2 \, dx\right)\right]$$

Since $I > 0$, we have:
$$\left(\int_0^1 x f(x)^2 \, dx\right)\left(\int_0^1 f(x) \, dx\right) > \left(\int_0^1 x f(x) \, dx\right)\left(\int_0^1 f(x)^2 \, dx\right)$$

Dividing both sides by the positive quantities $\int_0^1 f(x) \, dx$ and $\int_0^1 f(x)^2 \, dx$:
$$\frac{\int_0^1 x f(x)^2 \, dx}{\int_0^1 f(x)^2 \, dx} > \frac{\int_0^1 x f(x) \, dx}{\int_0^1 f(x) \, dx}$$

Therefore, $x_2 > x_1$.

## Intuition

The key insight is that when we square $f(x)$, we amplify the larger values of $f$ more than the smaller values. Since $f$ is strictly increasing, the larger values occur at larger $x$. Thus, squaring $f$ increases the relative weight toward $x = 1$, shifting the centroid to the right.

The double integral proof formalizes this "rearrangement" effect: it shows that the correlation between position $x$ and weight $f(x)$ becomes stronger when we use $f(x)^2$ instead of $f(x)$ as the weight function.
