# Putnam 2025 B3 - Solution

## Answer

**YES**, $S$ must contain all positive integers.

## Solution

We prove by strong induction that all positive integers are in $S$.

### Step 1: Base Case and Key Factorization

Since $S$ is nonempty, pick any $n_0 \in S$. Since $1 \mid 2025^{n_0} - 15^{n_0}$, we have $1 \in S$.

**Factorization:** For any $n \geq 1$:
$$2025^n - 15^n = 15^n(135^n - 1) = 3^n \cdot 5^n \cdot (135^n - 1)$$
where $135 = 2025/15 = 3^3 \cdot 5$.

### Step 2: Key Divisibility Lemma

**Lemma:** For any $n \geq 1$, write $n = 3^a \cdot 5^b \cdot m$ where $\gcd(m, 15) = 1$. Then $n \mid 2025^k - 15^k$ if and only if:
1. $k \geq \max(a, b)$, and
2. $\mathrm{ord}_m(135) \mid k$

where $\mathrm{ord}_m(135)$ is the multiplicative order of $135$ modulo $m$ (with $\mathrm{ord}_1(135) = 1$).

**Proof:** We have $2025^k - 15^k = 3^k \cdot 5^k \cdot (135^k - 1)$.

Since $135 = 3^3 \cdot 5 \equiv 0 \pmod{3}$ and $135 \equiv 0 \pmod{5}$, we have $\gcd(3, 135^k - 1) = \gcd(3, -1) = 1$ and $\gcd(5, 135^k - 1) = 1$.

Therefore:
- $3^a \mid 2025^k - 15^k$ iff $3^a \mid 3^k$, i.e., $k \geq a$.
- $5^b \mid 2025^k - 15^k$ iff $5^b \mid 5^k$, i.e., $k \geq b$.
- $m \mid 2025^k - 15^k$ iff $m \mid 135^k - 1$ (since $\gcd(m, 15) = 1$), i.e., $\mathrm{ord}_m(135) \mid k$.

### Step 3: Main Induction

**Claim:** For any integer $n \geq 2$, there exists $k$ with $1 \leq k < n$ such that $n \mid 2025^k - 15^k$.

**Proof:** Write $n = 3^a \cdot 5^b \cdot m$ where $\gcd(m, 15) = 1$. By the Lemma, we need $k \geq \max(a, b)$ and $\mathrm{ord}_m(135) \mid k$.

Let $d = \mathrm{ord}_m(135)$. The smallest valid $k$ is:
$$k_0 = d \cdot \left\lceil \frac{\max(a, b)}{d} \right\rceil$$

We show $k_0 < n$.

**Case 1:** $m = 1$ (i.e., $n = 3^a \cdot 5^b$ with $a + b \geq 1$).

Then $d = \mathrm{ord}_1(135) = 1$, so $k_0 = \max(a, b)$.

We need $\max(a, b) < 3^a \cdot 5^b$. This holds since:
- If $a \geq 1$: $3^a \geq 3 > a$, and $3^a \cdot 5^b \geq 3^a > a \geq \max(a, b)$ when $a \geq b$.
- If $b \geq 1$ and $b > a$: $5^b \geq 5 > b$ for $b \leq 2$, and $5^b$ grows faster than $b$ for $b \geq 3$.

**Case 2:** $m \geq 2$.

We have $k_0 \leq \max(a, b) + d - 1 \leq \max(a, b) + \phi(m) - 1 < \max(a, b) + m$.

We need $\max(a, b) + m \leq n = 3^a \cdot 5^b \cdot m$, i.e., $\max(a, b) \leq m(3^a \cdot 5^b - 1)$.

- If $a = b = 0$: LHS $= 0$, RHS $= m \cdot 0 = 0$. But then $n = m \geq 2$ and $k_0 = d = \mathrm{ord}_m(135) \leq \phi(m) < m = n$.

- If $a + b \geq 1$: Then $3^a \cdot 5^b \geq 3$, so $m(3^a \cdot 5^b - 1) \geq 2 \cdot 2 = 4$.
  Also, $\max(a, b) \leq \log_3(3^a \cdot 5^b) < 3^a \cdot 5^b - 1$ for $a + b \geq 1$.
  Thus $\max(a, b) < 3^a \cdot 5^b - 1 \leq m(3^a \cdot 5^b - 1)$.

In all cases, $k_0 < n$.

### Step 4: Completing the Induction

**Theorem:** All positive integers are in $S$.

**Proof by strong induction:**

*Base case:* $1 \in S$ (shown in Step 1).

*Inductive step:* Assume $\{1, 2, \ldots, n-1\} \subseteq S$. We prove $n \in S$.

By the Claim (Step 3), there exists $k$ with $1 \leq k < n$ such that $n \mid 2025^k - 15^k$.

By the inductive hypothesis, $k \in S$.

Since $k \in S$, all divisors of $2025^k - 15^k$ are in $S$.

Since $n \mid 2025^k - 15^k$, we have $n \in S$.

### Conclusion

By strong induction, $S$ contains all positive integers.
